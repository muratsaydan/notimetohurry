Rivea Beauty

Soap whose contents
you actually know.

I met Nehir through neighborhood chatter — nothing dramatic, just “there’s a girl nearby who actually makes the real thing.” At the time I was the one squinting at supermarket labels until my head spun; I had half-facts and a lot of “maybe.” When I knocked, laughter came from inside. I was still standing stiff on the doorstep; she had a sponge in her hand, said “come in,” and I stepped in. That was that.

The first visits felt a little like a polite standoff — who is this, what does she want. Nehir talks fast, laughs fast; I warm up slowly. Once we tried the soaps, the distance shrank. The scent is honest, and on the skin it doesn’t pick a fight with nonsense ingredients. Saying it out loud sounds bigger than it felt; mostly it smelled like a kitchen and someone had put the tea on. An ordinary afternoon.

Rivea Beauty didn’t arrive in one grand gesture. One evening on the balcony, while the tea went lukewarm, I asked why she didn’t sell them. She laughed. For weeks we circled the same topic: I kept nudging, she kept saying who would even buy it. In the end she said fine — but together. That was the word I wanted anyway; it just fell out that way. After that it turned practical: a name, colors, which oil, which ratio. I still read labels; Nehir mixes with her hands. Sometimes I ask myself what we’re really doing; the answer is usually just soap steam, nothing more staged than that.

Olive oil, coconut oil, shea butter — these aren’t “revolution” ingredients you roll out on TV. I don’t think of myself as living through especially deep moments there; most of the time it’s relief that they exist. If there is depth, it isn’t the kind I notice on purpose — maybe that’s better.
